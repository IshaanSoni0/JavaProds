================================================================================
  SECTION 1 — INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Genshin Impact is a third person action game where players build teams of
characters progress through the games story and clear combat challenges.
Over my three years of playing, I have spent a lot of time trying to make
my characters deal as much damage as possible to clear the hardest game
modes: Spiral Abyss, Imaginarium Theater, and Stygian Onslaught. This
process, typicaly called build optimisation, involves choosing the right
equipment for each character.

My main damage dealer is Neuvillette. What makes him interesting to
analyse is that his damage depends on several mechanics at once: a passive
ability that changes his damage bonus as his HP drops during an attack, an
artifact set that changes his critical hit rate as his HP fluctuates, and
a damage formula that scales directly with his maximum HP. Together, these
create a system where these mechanics interact, and all affect the final
damage.

Aim: The aim of this exploration has two parts: to model the probability
distribution of artifact substat rolls and determine what CRIT stats are
realistically achievable, and to use a mathematical damage model to find
the optimal CRIT Rate to CRIT DMG ratio and goblet main stat for
Neuvillette.

Notation used throughout the IA (all in decimal form unless stated):

  CR = CRIT Rate (e.g. 0.70 = 70%)
  CD = CRIT DMG (e.g. 1.40 = +140%)
  CV = CRIT Value, defined as CV = 2 x CR + CD
  k = weighting factor in CV (derived from roll means; k = 2)
  E[.] = expected value (average over randomness)
  P(.) = probability of an event
  N = number of artifacts farmed for one slot
  X = CV of one randomly rolled artifact piece
  X_i = CV of the i-th rolled artifact piece
  Y_N = max(X_1, ..., X_N), the best CV found after N pieces
  F(x) = empirical CDF of X, so F(x) = P(X <= x)
  M = talent multiplier per tick
  HP_max = maximum HP
  h = current HP fraction of maximum HP (0 <= h <= 1)
  K = product of non-crit terms in expected damage per tick
  A = product of fixed channel terms in Section 4
  DB_i = passive DMG Bonus at tick i
  B_fixed = non-passive DMG Bonus constant during one channel
  c = artifact CR contribution from substats
  d = artifact CD contribution from substats
  p = total HP% from all non-goblet sources
  B = total DMG Bonus from all non-goblet sources

================================================================================

  SECTION 2 — GAME MECHANICS OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Every hit in Genshin Impact follows the same general formula
(Genshin Impact Wiki, Damage):

  DMG = Base DMG × (1 + DMG Bonus) × DEF Multiplier
                 × RES Multiplier × CRIT Multiplier

Base DMG is the raw damage before any modifiers. For Neuvillette, his main
attack scales with his maximum HP, so:

  Base DMG = M × HP_max     (M = 12.51% per tick at talent level 8)

where M is the talent multiplier and HP_max is his total maximum HP.

DMG Bonus is the sum of all percentage damage bonuses from equipment and
passive abilities, expressed as a decimal (for example, +30% DMG Bonus
adds 0.30). These bonuses add together before being multiplied into the
formula.

DEF Multiplier accounts for the enemy's defence. The formula is
(Genshin Impact Wiki, Damage):

             Lc + 100
  DEF Mult = ─────────────────────────────
             (Le + 100) + (Lc + 100)

where Lc is the character's level and Le is the enemy's level.
For Neuvillette at level 80 fighting a level 100 enemy (standard for
endgame content like Spiral Abyss):

         180        180
  DEF = ─────── = ─────── ≈ 0.474
        200+180     380

RES Multiplier accounts for the enemy's elemental resistance. For a
typical enemy with 10% Hydro RES:

  RES Mult = 1 − 0.10 = 0.90

CRIT Multiplier is where CRIT Rate (CR) and CRIT DMG (CD) come in.
CRIT Rate is the probability that a hit deals a critical strike. CRIT DMG
is the percentage bonus to damage when a critical strike occurs. A
non-critical hit has a multiplier of 1; a critical hit has a multiplier
of (1 + CD). Since each hit is an independent event with success
probability CR, the expected value of the CRIT Multiplier is:

  E[CRIT Mult] = (1 − CR) × 1 + CR × (1 + CD) = 1 + CR × CD

Putting it all together, the expected damage per tick is:

  E[D_tick] = K × (1 + CR × CD)

  where  K = M × HP_max × (1 + DMG Bonus) × DEF Mult × RES Mult

K consists of every term that is fixed for a given build: the talent
scaling, the max HP, all additive damage bonuses, and both defensive
multipliers. Since these variables are not easly changeable, K is a
constant, and Optimising the artifact substats therefore reduces entirely
to maximising the crit multiplier.

The goblet comparison (Section 4.5) changes which goblet main stat is
used, which changes K since different goblets change either max HP or DMG
Bonus. But the CRIT optimisation that uses substats is unchanged
regardless of which goblet is equipped since the optimal CR and CD ratio
depends only on the substat budget, not on K. The two questions can
therefore be answered independently and combined at the end.

Neuvilette's passive talent, "Discipline of the Supreme Arbitration",
adds a Hydro DMG Bonus that depends on his current HP percentage. The
exact in-game text reads:

    "For each 1% of Neuvillette's current HP greater than 30% of Max HP,
     he will gain 0.6% Hydro DMG Bonus. A maximum bonus of 30% can be
     obtained this way."
    (Genshin Impact, in-game passive description)

Expressing this as a function of h, where h is the current percent of HP:

  PassiveBonus(h) = 0.006 × (100h − 30)   for 0.30 < h < 0.80
  PassiveBonus(h) = 0                      for h ≤ 0.30

The cap of 30% is reached at h = 0.80 (exactly 80% HP):

  0.006 × (80 − 30) = 0.30  ✓

During a full Charged Attack, HP drains from 100% down to approximately
50%. This creates two distinct phases for the passive:

  Phase 1 (100% → 80% HP):  Bonus is at its maximum of 30% throughout.
  Phase 2  (80% → 50% HP):  Bonus falls continuously from 30% to 12%.

Phase 2 will require integration over the attack duration to find the
average damage bonus.

Artifacts are the main equipment system in Genshin Impact. There are five
slots: Flower (always gives flat HP as its main stat), Plume (always gives
flat ATK), Sands, Goblet, and Circlet. Each piece also has up to four
substats chosen from a shared pool.

The Sands, Goblet, and Circlet have randomly assigned main stats, and
substats drawn from a weighted pool specific to each slot. Substats are
drawn sequentially from a pool of ten possible stats, excluding whichever
stat is already the main stat. Each substat has a weight that controls
the probability of being chosen (Genshin Impact Wiki, Artifact/Distribution):

  ┌──────────────────────┬────────┐
  │ Substat              │ Weight │
  ├──────────────────────┼────────┤
  │ Flat HP              │   6    │
  │ Flat ATK             │   6    │
  │ Flat DEF             │   6    │
  │ HP%                  │   4    │
  │ ATK%                 │   4    │
  │ DEF%                 │   4    │
  │ Energy Recharge%     │   4    │
  │ Elemental Mastery    │   4    │
  │ CRIT Rate%           │   3    │
  │ CRIT DMG%            │   3    │
  └──────────────────────┴────────┘

CRIT Rate% and CRIT DMG% each have the lowest weight in the pool. On a
Flower, for example, the total pool weight is 3+3+6+4+4+4+4+4+6+6 = 44
(flat HP excluded as main stat). The chance of any one draw being a
specific crit stat is only 3/44 ≈ 6.8%. Because only 4 substats are
chosen, most artifact pieces will not have both CRIT Rate and CRIT DMG
as substats.

The four substat draws happen without replacement. Once a substat is
chosen, it is removed from the pool for that piece. Each time a substat
is rolled (either at the start or during an upgrade), it lands on one of
four value tiers with equal probability (25% each). For CRIT Rate% and
CRIT DMG%, the tiers are (Genshin Impact Wiki, Artifact/Stats):

  ┌────────┬────────────┬────────────┐
  │  Tier  │ CRIT Rate% │ CRIT DMG%  │
  ├────────┼────────────┼────────────┤
  │   1    │   3.89%    │   7.77%    │
  │   2    │   3.50%    │   7.00%    │
  │   3    │   3.11%    │   6.22%    │
  │   4    │   2.72%    │   5.44%    │
  └────────┴────────────┴────────────┘

Every CRIT DMG% tier value is exactly twice the corresponding CRIT Rate%
tier value. The average roll for each stat is:

  avrCR = (3.89 + 3.50 + 3.11 + 2.72) / 4 = 13.22 / 4 = 3.305%
  avrCD = (7.77 + 7.00 + 6.22 + 5.44) / 4 = 26.43 / 4 = 6.608%

So avrCD = 2 × avrCR exactly. This ratio is how the CRIT Value metric
is defined.

An artifact starts with either 3 substats (80% of pieces) or 4 substats
(20%) and is upgraded 5 events total. At each event, one existing substat
is selected at random (each substat has a 25% chance) and receives one
additional roll at a random tier. For pieces that start with 3 substats,
the first event instead adds the fourth substat from the remaining
weighted pool.

Because each upgrade picks uniformly from 4 substats, the number of times
any given substat gets upgraded follows a Binomial distribution:
BinomPDF(5, 0.25, X). This makes hitting a high CRIT value on any single
piece unlikly, which is exactly what the simulation in Section 3 shows.

Marechaussee Hunter is the recommended artifact set for Neuvillette. Its
bonuses are (Genshin Impact, in-game set description):

  2-piece: Normal and Charged Attack DMG +15%
  4-piece: "When current HP increases or decreases, CRIT Rate will be
            increased by 12% for 5s. Max 3 stacks."

The 4-piece bonus interacts with Neuvillette's HP drain mechanic. Each
hit of his Charged Attack reduces his current HP, which counts as an HP
change event. This means all three stacks are made and maintained
throughout the attack, adding +36% CRIT Rate to his effective total, and
This +0.36 contribution from the set is accounted for in the optimisation
in Section 4.

3.1  Probability of Double-Crit Substats
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Each substat is drawn one at a time from the weighted pool, without
replacement. The probability of drawing a specific stat on a given draw
equals its weight divided by the total remaining weight at that moment.

Step 1: probability of one specific ordering

Take a Flower as a worked example. Flat HP is always the main stat and is
excluded from the pool, leaving nine substats with total weight W = 38.
Both CR% and CD% have weight 3.

What is the probability that CR% is drawn first and CD% is drawn second?

  P(CR% 1st) = 3/38

After CR% is drawn, its weight of 3 is removed from the pool.
The remaining pool weight is 38 − 3 = 35.

  P(CD% 2nd | CR% already drawn) = 3/35

  P(CR% 1st AND CD% 2nd) = 3/38 × 3/35 = 9/1330 ≈ 0.68%

This covers only one specific ordering of the four draws. In reality CR%
and CD% can land in any two of the four substat slots, with any two other
stats filling the remaining slots. The two "filler" substats are drawn
from the seven non-crit stats: flat ATK and flat DEF (weight 6 each) and
HP%, ATK%, DEF%, ER%, EM (weight 4 each).

Step 2: summing over all valid orderings

A piece has 4 substat slots filled one at a time. Once we fix which 4
stats appear — say {CR%, CD%, flat ATK, HP%} — those 4 stats can arrive
in any order across the 4 draws. The number of ways to arrange 4 distinct
items in a sequence is 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 4! = 24 orderings.

For every set of four substats that includes both CR% and CD%, each of
those 24 orderings must be evaluated using the same sequential probability
method as Step 1, and their probabilities summed. When the crits appear
in later positions, a filler stat has been drawn first, so the denominator
at the crit's draw is smaller (the pool has already shrunk). This is what
makes the sum non-trivial.

The next question is: which sets of 4 substats can contain both CR% and
CD%? The other 2 slots must be filled by 2 of the 7 non-crit stats. The
number of ways to choose 2 stats from 7 (where only the pair matters, not
which comes first) is:

  C(7,2) = 7! / (2! × 5!) = (7 × 6) / 2 = 21 possible filler pairs

The 7 non-crit stats fall into two weight tiers:

  Weight 6: flat ATK, flat DEF                  (2 stats)
  Weight 4: HP%, ATK%, DEF%, ER%, EM            (5 stats)

A filler pair can be drawn from these tiers in three distinct ways:

  Class (6,6): both fillers from the weight-6 tier
               C(2,2) = 1 pair:  {flat ATK, flat DEF}

  Class (6,4): one filler from each tier
               2 × 5 = 10 pairs:  e.g. {flat ATK, HP%}, {flat DEF, ER%}, ...

  Class (4,4): both fillers from the weight-4 tier
               C(5,2) = (5 × 4) / 2 = 10 pairs:  e.g. {HP%, ATK%}, {ER%, EM}, ...

  Total: 1 + 10 + 10 = 21 pairs ✓

Why group by weight class? In the sequential draw formula, the probability
of a given ordering depends only on the weights drawn at each step, not on
which specific stat was chosen. For example, the ordering where CR% comes
2nd and HP% comes 1st gives the same probability as CR% 2nd and DEF% 1st,
because HP% and DEF% share the same weight of 4. Every pair within a class
therefore produces the same total probability when you sum its 24 orderings,
so:

  class contribution = (pairs in class) × (24-ordering sum for one pair)

For class (6,6) — 1 pair {flat ATK, flat DEF}:
Two of the 24 orderings, to show why the denominators differ:

  CR% 1st, CD% 2nd, flat ATK 3rd, flat DEF 4th:
    P = 3/38 × 3/35 × 6/32 × 6/26

  flat ATK 1st, CR% 2nd, CD% 3rd, flat DEF 4th:
    P = 6/38 × 3/32 × 3/29 × 6/26

Drawing a weight-6 stat first removes 6 from the pool and shifts all
subsequent denominators down. Summing all 24 orderings for this one pair
gives 0.8718%. With only 1 pair in the class:

  Class (6,6) contribution = 1 × 0.8718% = 0.8718%

For class (6,4) — 10 pairs:
Summing all 24 orderings for one representative pair (e.g. {flat ATK, HP%})
gives 0.5188%. The same sum holds for all 10 pairs in this class:

  Class (6,4) contribution = 10 × 0.5188% = 5.1881%

For class (4,4) — 10 pairs:
One representative pair (e.g. {HP%, ATK%}) contributes 0.3096%:

  Class (4,4) contribution = 10 × 0.3096% = 3.0964%

Total double-crit probability for a Flower:

  P = 0.8718% + 5.1881% + 3.0964% = 9.1562%

Because evaluating all 21 × 24 = 504 ordered sequences by hand would be
prone to arithmetic error, these sums were verified exhaustively using a
Python script (see Appendix A). The analytical result of 9.1562% matches
the simulation to within 0.01%.

The Plume has an identical pool (W = 38, same non-crit weight
distribution), giving the same analytical result; the simulation's 9.18%
differs only by sampling noise. For other slot types, removing a
different main stat changes the pool, so probabilities differ.
Hydro DMG Bonus% does not exist as a substat, so a Goblet with that main
excludes nothing extra — its pool has W = 44, every fractional weight is
smaller, and the double-crit probability falls to 7.69%.

  Table 1: Double-crit probability by slot
  ┌──────────────────────────────┬────┬────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Slot / main stat             │  W │  P(double-crit)                │
  ├──────────────────────────────┼────┼────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Flower (flat HP main)        │ 38 │   9.16%                        │
  │ Plume (flat ATK main)        │ 38 │   9.16%                        │
  │ Sands (HP% main)             │ 40 │   8.34%                        │
  │ Goblet (Hydro DMG% main)     │ 44 │   6.69%                        │
  │ Circlet (CD% main)           │ 41 │   0%                           │
  └──────────────────────────────┴────┴────────────────────────────────┘

For Neuvillette's Circlet, CD% is the main stat and is excluded from the
substat pool entirely. Only CR% remains as a possible crit substat, so
P(double-crit | CD% main) = 0% exactly regardless of how many pieces
are farmed.

3.2  Upgrade Roll Distribution
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Once the four substats are set, the piece gains five upgrade events at
+4, +8, +12, +16, and +20. At each event, one of the four substats is
selected uniformly at random (25% each) and receives one additional roll.

The number of upgrades a specific substat receives over five events
follows a Binomial distribution which is shown in table 2:

  X ~ Bin(5, 0.25)

  Table 2: Upgrade count distribution for one substat  [X ~ Bin(5, 0.25)]
  ┌───────────────────┬─────────────┐
  │ Upgrades received │ Probability │
  ├───────────────────┼─────────────┤
  │        0          │   23.73%    │
  │        1          │   39.55%    │
  │        2          │   26.37%    │
  │        3          │    8.79%    │
  │        4          │    1.46%    │
  │        5          │    0.10%    │
  └───────────────────┴─────────────┘

therefore, if an artifact has 2 crit substats, on any given roll there is a 50%
chance that a crit stat is upgraded. the probability of Getting 3 or more upgrades
into crit Substats which is the typical standard for a strong artifact happens
50% of the time as shown by adding up the chances of rolling into crit 3, 4 and 5 times

BinomPDF(5,0.5,3) + BinomPDF(5,0.5,4) + BinomPDF(5,0.5,5) = 0.5

Combined with the ~9.2% chance of double-crit in the first place (for a
Flower), this means a Flower with at least 3 upgrades in crit
substats occurs in about 9.2% × 50% ≈ 4.6% of pieces. This shows
that high CV on a single piece is genuinely rare.

3.3  5 million artifact Simulation and CV Distribution
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Based on all the game mechanichs previously mentioned I made a program to simulate any number of artifacts
and to record their stats. in the simulation, CR% and CD% substat values are recorded per piece in order to 
confirm the mathamatical model of artifact rolling.

Crit Value (CV) metric:

To combine CR% and CD% into a single score, define:

  CV = k × CR% + CD%

where k weights CR% relative to CD%. From Section 2, the average substat
roll values are avrCR = 3.305% and avrCD = 6.608%, giving:

  k = avrCD / avrCR = 6.608 / 3.305 = 2.000

So CV = 2 × CR% + CD%. This metric counts crit budget in CD%-equivalent
units: one expected CR roll is worth exactly half of one expected CD roll,
and the constraint CV = 2 × CR + CD represents the roll investment. 
any two builds with the same CV represent the same total number of crit substat value.

Key findings  (5,000,000 artifact simultion per peice)

P(CV = 0) — no crit substats at all:

  Flower / Plume:      ~40.9%
  Sands / Goblet:      ~47.9%
  Circlet (CD% main):  ~68%

On average, only about 1 in 2 pieces has any crit value. For a CD% main
Circlet, 2 in 3 pieces have no crit substats at all, since the only
available crit substat is CR%.

Mean CV (substat CV only, all pieces including CV = 0):

  Flower / Plume: ~9.26     Sands: ~8.89
  Goblet: ~8.53             Circlet (CD% main): ~7.89

These low means are largely driven by the ~40–68% of pieces with CV = 0.
Among pieces with at least one crit substat, mean CV rises to ~15.2–15.6
for Flower, Plume, Sands, and Goblet.

Along with this, the probability for a peice to have 2 crit substats was empericly recorded.

3.4  Realistic Crit Constraint from Farming
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Rather than setting an arbitrary CV threshold for "a good piece", this
section derives the expected best CV achievable with a fixed farming
investment. The expected-maximum function E[max of N independent samples]
reads directly off a simulation curve: given N attempts, it gives the
expected highest CV found.

Farming benchmark

A typical two-week farming period yields:

  14 days × 200 resin/day = 2,800 resin
  ÷ 20 resin per 5-star artifact      ≈ 140 pieces total
  ÷ 5 slot types                      ≈ 28 pieces per slot
  → rounded to N = 30 per slot

Table 3 shows the expected best substat-only CV per slot at N = 30,
computed from the simulation's empirical CDF (see Appendix B):

To make this precise, let X be the CV of one randomly dropped piece for a
given slot, and let F(x) = P(X <= x) be its empirical CDF from the
simulation. If N pieces are farmed independently with CV values
X_1, X_2, ..., X_N, define Y_N = max(X_1, ..., X_N), i.e. the best CV
obtained after N rolls. Then

  P(Y_N <= x) = P(X_1 <= x, ..., X_N <= x) = [F(x)]^N

So the expected-best-value chart is the graph of E[Y_N] against N, where
E[Y_N] is evaluated numerically from this max-distribution using the
simulation CV bins. Intuitively, increasing N shifts [F(x)]^N to the right,
so expected best CV increases but with diminishing returns.

At N = 30, the chart values are read for each slot and reported in Table 3.
These are not peak outliers; they are expectation values of the best piece
after 30 attempts per slot.

  Table 3: Expected best CV per slot at N = 30 pieces
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┬──────────────────┐
  │ Slot                                     │ E[max CV @ N=30] │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────┤
  │ Flower                                   │      32.5        │
  │ Plume                                    │      32.5        │
  │ Sands                                    │      32.0        │
  │ Goblet                                   │      32.0        │
  │ Circlet (CD% main; CR% substat only)     │      31.0        │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────┴──────────────────┘

The Circlet's lower expected maximum reflects the CD% main constraint:
with only CR% available as a crit substat, the CV ceiling is lower.

Total substat CV budget across all five pieces:

  32.5 + 32.5 + 32.0 + 32.0 + 31.0 = 160

The Circlet's CD% main stat contributes a fixed +62.2% to CD at +20 on a
5-star piece. This is not a substat roll — it is guaranteed regardless of
RNG.

  Total_CV = 160

This value of Total_CV = 160, combined with k = 2 derived in Section 3.3,
is the constraint used in the optimisation in Section 4.

4. DAMAGE FUNCTION AND CRIT OPTIMISATION                     (~4–5 pages)

   4.1  Full expected damage over one Charged Attack

The objective in this section is to optimise expected damage over one full
Charged Attack channel, not a single tick, because Neuvillette's HP-dependent
passive changes during the attack.

From gameplay observations used in this IA:
  - one full channel lasts 3.0 s,
  - it deals 8 damage ticks,
  - HP decreases 5 times (once every 0.5 s),
  - each HP-loss event is 8% of max HP.

Define A as the product of terms that are fixed during one channel:

  A = M x HP_max x DEF_mult x RES_mult

Let B_fixed be all non-passive DMG Bonus terms that stay constant during the
channel (for example, goblet bonus and the Marechaussee 2-piece +15% CA bonus).
Let DB_i be the passive DMG Bonus at tick i. Then expected damage at tick i is:

  E[D_i] = A x (1 + B_fixed + DB_i) x (1 + CR_eff x CD)

So total expected damage over one Charged Attack is:

  E[D_CA] = sum(i=1 to 8) E[D_i]
          = A x (1 + CR_eff x CD) x sum(i=1 to 8)(1 + B_fixed + DB_i)

This is the function to optimise in Section 4.4.

   4.2  Passive talent term across the 3-second channel

The passive from Section 2 is:

  PassiveBonus(h) = min(0.006 x (100h - 30), 0.30), for h > 0.30

with h as current HP fraction of max HP.

Using the 5 HP-loss events of 8% each, HP during the six 0.5-second intervals is:

  100%, 92%, 84%, 76%, 68%, 60%

So passive bonus values are:

  DB = 30.0%, 30.0%, 30.0%, 27.6%, 22.8%, 18.0%

because for h > 80% the passive is capped at 30%, and below 80% it follows
0.6% per 1% HP above 30%.

Since each interval has equal length (0.5 s), the channel-average passive bonus is:

  DB_avg = (30.0 + 30.0 + 30.0 + 27.6 + 22.8 + 18.0)/6
         = 26.4%

Therefore an equivalent compact form for channel expectation is:

  E[D_CA] = 8 x A x (1 + B_fixed + 0.264) x (1 + CR_eff x CD)

This keeps the full-channel dependence on HP fluctuation, while reducing the
expression to a constant passive factor for optimisation.

   4.3  Marechaussee Hunter CR term during the channel

For this model, Marechaussee Hunter 4-piece is already fully stacked before the
Charged Attack starts (3 pre-heals), and then maintained during the full channel.
So CRIT Rate gain is constant at +36% for all 8 ticks.

Let c be artifact CR (decimal form), and let base character CR be 0.05.
Then effective CR during the whole channel is:

  CR_eff = c + 0.05 + 0.36 = c + 0.41

So unlike earlier piecewise drafts, CR_eff is not time-dependent in this final
model; only the passive bonus term varies over the channel.

   4.4  Crit rate vs crit damage optimisation under CV constraint

From Section 3.4, Total_CV = 160 (substat CV only) and from Section 3.3,
k = 2. Using decimal stats, this gives the artifact-substat constraint:

  2c + d = 1.60

where c = artifact CR from substats and d = artifact CD from substats.

However, the crit multiplier must use total CD, not only substat CD.
Two fixed CD terms are added to every build in this model:

  CD_circlet_main = 0.622
  CD_base_level80 = 0.788

So fixed CD is:

  CD_fixed = 0.622 + 0.788 = 1.410

Rearrange:

  d = 1.60 - 2c

In E[D_CA], the only term depending on c and d is crit factor:

  F(c) = 1 + CR_eff x CD_total
       = 1 + (c + 0.41)(d + CD_fixed)
       = 1 + (c + 0.41)(1.60 - 2c + 1.410)
       = 1 + (c + 0.41)(3.01 - 2c)

Expand:

  F(c) = 2.2341 + 2.19c - 2c^2

Differentiate and set equal to zero:

  F'(c) = 2.19 - 4c = 0
  c* = 0.5475

Second derivative:

  F''(c) = -4 < 0

so this critical point is a maximum.

Now compute corresponding CD values:

  d* = 1.60 - 2(0.5475) = 0.505  (artifact substat CD)
  CD_total* = d* + 1.410 = 1.915

So the optimal artifact distribution is:

  artifact CR* = 54.75%
  artifact CD* (substats) = 50.5%
  total CD* (including base + circlet main) = 191.5%

and effective CR during the channel is:

  CR_eff* = 0.5475 + 0.41 = 0.9575 = 95.75%

Boundary check:

  c <= 1 - 0.05 - 0.36 = 0.59

Since c* = 0.5475 <= 0.59, the optimum is still interior and feasible
(but close to the upper CR boundary).

The resulting effective crit ratio is 95.75 : 191.5, which is exactly a 1 : 2 ratio which the comunity generaly holds
as a rule of thumb when building charecters, however Here this ratio is derived from the constrained crit value
optimisation with fixed base and main-stat terms included.

   4.5  Goblet comparison: Hydro DMG% vs HP%

With c* and d* fixed from 4.4, crit factor is fixed. Therefore goblet choice
only changes the non-crit HP/DMG term:

  (M x maxHP) x (1 + total DMG Bonus)

Define:

  p = max HP% from all sources EXCEPT goblet,
  B = total DMG Bonus from all sources EXCEPT goblet,

where B includes the channel-average passive term from 4.2.
Use M = 0.1251 (talent multiplier per tick) and let HP denote base max HP
before percentage bonuses.

Then:

  Hydro goblet multiplier  ~  (0.1251(HP x (1 + p)))(1 + B + 0.466)
  HP% goblet multiplier    ~  (0.1251(HP x (1 + p + 0.466)))(1 + B)

To find the break-even point, set these equal:

  (0.1251(HP x (1 + p + 0.466)))(1 + B)
    = (0.1251(HP x (1 + p)))(1 + B + 0.466)

Expanding both sides and cancelling common terms gives:

  (1 + p + 0.466)(1 + B) = (1 + p)(1 + B + 0.466)
  0.466(1 + B) = 0.466(1 + p)
  B = p

So the decision rule is:

  - if B > p, HP% goblet gives higher damage,
  - if B < p, Hydro goblet gives higher damage,
  - if B = p, they are equal.

In this model, passive average is fixed at 0.264 and Marechaussee 2-piece gives
another 0.15 Charged Attack DMG, so even before counting other buffs:

  B_min = 0.264 + 0.15 = 0.414

Representative numerical check including HP% Sands (+46.6%):

  p = 0.466, B = 0.414

  Hydro multiplier  = (1 + 0.466)(1 + 0.414 + 0.466)
                    = 1.466 x 1.880 = 2.7561
  HP% multiplier    = (1 + 0.466 + 0.466)(1 + 0.414)
                    = 1.932 x 1.414 = 2.7318

Relative gain of HP% over Hydro:

  2.7318 / 2.7561 - 1 = -0.0088 = -0.88%

So in this check, Hydro DMG% is about 0.88% stronger than HP%.

Interpretation: this matches the break-even rule B = p. With HP% Sands,
p = 0.466 exceeds B = 0.414, so adding +46.6% DMG Bonus (Hydro goblet)
provides the larger marginal gain.

5. CONCLUSION                                                (~0.5–1 page)

  5.1  Summary of findings

This exploration achieved its stated aim by combining a game-mechanics
damage model with probability and optimisation.

First, the expected damage model was built from first principles and then
adapted to one full Neuvillette Charged Attack channel (8 ticks over 3 s),
including HP-dependent passive variation. Using the discrete HP sequence
100%, 92%, 84%, 76%, 68%, 60%, the channel-average passive bonus was
calculated as 26.4%.

Second, the goblet comparison was solved analytically by setting Hydro DMG%
and HP% goblet multipliers equal and deriving the break-even condition B = p.
In the representative check including HP% Sands (p = 0.466) and
B = 0.414 from passive + set bonus, Hydro DMG% produced about a 0.88%
higher multiplier than HP%.

Third, realistic crit constraints were obtained from artifact RNG rather than
from an arbitrary target. The simulation-based expected-maximum method at
N = 30 pieces per slot gave an estimated total substat budget of
Total_CV = 160.

Finally, with k = 2 and the constraint 2c + d = 1.60, optimisation of the
crit factor gave c* = 0.5475 and d* = 0.505, which corresponds to artifact
CR = 54.75% and artifact CD = 50.5%. Including fixed CD (base + circlet main),
the build's total CD is 191.5% and effective CR during the channel is 95.75%,
which is an exact 1:2 effective crit ratio.

  5.2  Limitations and significance

The main limitation is that this is a controlled model, not a full combat
simulator. Enemy resistance, defence, rotations, and external buffs are held
fixed to isolate the artifact optimisation problem. This means the absolute
damage numbers are context-dependent, but the key optimisation structure is
still meaningful: DEF and RES primarily scale the constant term and do not
change the internal CV trade-off used to derive the crit split.

A second limitation is the Marechaussee Hunter stack assumption. The model
assumes full pre-stacking (+36% CR) for the full channel. In practical play,
there may be a short ramp-up window (roughly 0.5 s of a 3 s channel, about
17% of ticks) where CR is lower. This can reduce realised damage slightly,
but is unlikely to reverse the overall conclusion because the optimisation
remains close to the same CR/CD balance under nearby CR shifts.

A third limitation is that the farming benchmark uses a stylised investment
(N = 30 per slot over two weeks). Real players distribute resin unevenly and
often keep farming beyond that horizon. Therefore Total_CV = 160 should be
read as a realistic benchmark for the stated scenario, not a universal cap.
Higher investment would shift the expected-max curve upward.

The decision to omit a full community-guide comparison is a scope limitation
introduced by IA length constraints. To compensate, this conclusion explicitly
evaluates limitations and explains where the results are robust versus where
they depend on assumptions.

Overall significance: this investigation shows that a common build question
can be reduced to a transparent mathematical framework. The model explains
not only what the recommended stat balance is under stated assumptions, but
also why that balance emerges from RNG constraints and multiplicative damage
structure.

  5.3  Extensions

Natural extensions are to (i) rerun the expected-max model with personal
artifact logs instead of generic simulation distributions, (ii) incorporate
full rotation-level team damage, and (iii) test sensitivity of goblet and
crit conclusions across enemy resistance and different buff environments.
These would improve external validity while preserving the core method.
